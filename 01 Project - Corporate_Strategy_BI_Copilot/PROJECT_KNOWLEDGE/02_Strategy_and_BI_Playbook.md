# Strategy and Business Intelligence Playbook

**Project:** Corporate Strategy & Business Intelligence Copilot  
**Version:** 1.0  
**Purpose:** Provide a compact, repeatable method for strategy, performance analysis, market intelligence, financial assessment, and decision support.

---

## 1. Framework Selection Guide

Use the smallest set of methods needed to answer the business question.

| Business question | Primary method | Supporting method |
|---|---|---|
| Why is performance changing? | KPI variance and driver analysis | Root-cause analysis |
| What is the company’s strategic position? | SWOT | PESTLE or Five Forces |
| Is an industry or market attractive? | Porter’s Five Forces | PESTLE and market sizing |
| How do we compare with competitors? | Competitor comparison | Customer-value analysis |
| Which option should management choose? | Weighted decision matrix | Scenario and risk analysis |
| Should we invest? | Business case and cash-flow analysis | Sensitivity analysis |
| What should be done first? | Impact–effort prioritisation | Dependency review |
| How should a strategy be executed? | Objective–initiative–KPI roadmap | Risk and governance review |

A framework organises thinking; it does not make the decision. Recommendations must also reflect economics, capabilities, timing, risk, and evidence quality.

# Part I — Core Strategy Analysis

## 2. Strategy Framing

Before selecting a framework, define:

1. **Objective:** What outcome is management seeking?
2. **Scope:** Which products, customers, markets, functions, and period are included?
3. **Current position:** What is happening now?
4. **Strategic gap:** What separates the current position from the desired outcome?
5. **Decision:** What must management choose, stop, start, or accelerate?
6. **Constraints:** What limits capital, time, capability, risk, or compliance?
7. **Success measure:** How will management know the strategy is working?

A useful strategy statement links **where to play**, **how to win**, **required capabilities**, and **measurable outcomes**.

## 3. SWOT Analysis

### Use when

A concise integrated view of internal and external factors is required.

### Method

| Internal | Positive | Negative |
|---|---|---|
| Organisation | Strengths: capabilities or assets that create advantage | Weaknesses: internal limitations that reduce performance |

| External | Positive | Negative |
|---|---|---|
| Environment | Opportunities: external conditions the company can exploit | Threats: external conditions that could harm results |

### Quality rules

- Make each item specific and evidence-based.
- Do not describe the same issue as both a strength and an opportunity.
- Distinguish current capabilities from aspirations.
- Limit each quadrant to the most material three to five items.
- Convert the matrix into actions.

### Convert SWOT into action

- **Use strengths to capture opportunities.**
- **Use strengths to reduce threats.**
- **Correct weaknesses that block attractive opportunities.**
- **Reduce exposure where weaknesses and threats combine.**

### Output

End with three to five strategic implications, not only a four-box list.

## 4. PESTLE Analysis

### Use when

Macro-environmental change may materially affect demand, cost, operations, or risk.

| Dimension | Questions |
|---|---|
| Political | Could government priorities, trade policy, public spending, or stability affect the business? |
| Economic | How could growth, inflation, interest rates, exchange rates, income, or unemployment affect demand and cost? |
| Social | Are demographics, preferences, trust, lifestyles, or workforce expectations changing? |
| Technological | Which technologies could improve, disrupt, substitute, or regulate the offering? |
| Legal | Which laws, licences, contracts, employment rules, or data obligations matter? |
| Environmental | Which climate, resource, waste, energy, or sustainability factors affect the model? |

### Method

For each material factor, state:

`Factor → Direction of change → Time horizon → Business exposure → Opportunity or risk → Recommended response`

Avoid a generic catalogue. Prioritise factors by impact and likelihood.

## 5. Porter’s Five Forces

### Use when

Assessing industry profitability, competitive pressure, or market-entry attractiveness.

| Force | Key questions | Strong-force indicators |
|---|---|---|
| Rivalry | How many credible competitors exist? Is growth slow? Are offerings undifferentiated? | Price pressure, high fixed costs, frequent switching |
| New entrants | How difficult is it to enter at scale? | Low capital, weak regulation, easy distribution access |
| Buyer power | Can customers force lower prices or higher service? | Concentrated buyers, low switching costs, transparent pricing |
| Supplier power | Can suppliers raise cost or restrict supply? | Few suppliers, scarce inputs, high switching costs |
| Substitutes | Can customers solve the same need differently? | Better price–performance, low switching cost, rapid innovation |

### Output

Rate each force **Low, Medium, or High**, explain the evidence, and conclude:

- likely margin pressure;
- defensible sources of advantage;
- entry or investment implications; and
- practical countermeasures.

## 6. Strategic Options

Develop genuinely different options rather than minor variations of one idea. Typical options include:

- deepen the core market;
- enter a new customer segment;
- expand geographically;
- develop or acquire a new offering;
- partner or outsource;
- improve productivity before pursuing growth; or
- maintain the current approach while gathering evidence.

For each option, specify:

- target outcome;
- value-creation logic;
- customer or market affected;
- required capabilities;
- investment and time;
- principal risks; and
- conditions for success.

## 7. Weighted Decision Matrix

### Method

1. Select five to seven decision criteria.
2. Assign weights totalling 100%.
3. Score each option, normally from 1 to 5.
4. Calculate `Weighted score = Weight × Score`.
5. Explain the evidence behind the scores.
6. test whether changing a major weight alters the result.

### Suggested criteria

| Criterion | Example weight |
|---|---:|
| Strategic alignment | 20% |
| Market or customer impact | 20% |
| Financial attractiveness | 20% |
| Execution feasibility | 15% |
| Time to benefit | 10% |
| Risk and reversibility | 15% |
| **Total** | **100%** |

Do not present the highest score as an automatic answer. Consider non-quantifiable constraints and the sensitivity of the ranking.

## 8. Strategy Execution Chain

Translate strategy using:

`Strategic objective → Initiative → Owner → Milestone → KPI → Target → Risk → Next action`

A strong initiative should have:

- a clear business outcome;
- one accountable owner;
- measurable milestones;
- leading and lagging indicators;
- dependencies;
- an escalation threshold; and
- a review cadence.

# Part II — Business Intelligence and Performance Analysis

## 9. BI Analysis Cycle

Use this sequence:

1. **Question:** Define the business decision or performance issue.
2. **Data:** Confirm source, period, unit, coverage, and definition.
3. **Quality:** Check completeness, consistency, duplicates, and outliers.
4. **Comparison:** Compare actuals with prior period, target, budget, forecast, or peers.
5. **Segmentation:** Break results by product, region, customer, channel, cohort, or process stage.
6. **Drivers:** Identify factors that mathematically or operationally explain the result.
7. **Implication:** State what the result means for growth, margin, cash, customers, or risk.
8. **Action:** Recommend a response and measure of success.

## 10. Insight Ladder

A management insight should progress through the ladder:

- **Observation:** Revenue was 6% below budget.
- **Trend:** The shortfall widened for the third consecutive month.
- **Driver:** Enterprise conversion and average deal size both declined.
- **Implication:** The annual growth target is at risk even if lead volume recovers.
- **Action:** Requalify the pipeline, review pricing exceptions, and focus executive support on late-stage enterprise deals.
- **Measure:** Conversion rate, average deal size, and weighted pipeline coverage.

Stop at the highest level justified by the evidence. Label a driver as a hypothesis when it has not been confirmed.

## 11. KPI Variance Analysis

For each material KPI, review:

| Element | Question |
|---|---|
| Current result | What was achieved? |
| Reference | What was the target, budget, forecast, or prior result? |
| Absolute variance | How many units or currency units above or below? |
| Relative variance | What percentage above or below? |
| Direction and trend | Is performance improving, deteriorating, or volatile? |
| Driver | Which volume, price, mix, cost, conversion, retention, or timing factor explains it? |
| Implication | Why does it matter? |
| Action | What should be done, by whom, and by when? |

### Useful decompositions

- **Revenue:** customers or units × average price; or traffic × conversion × average order value.
- **Gross profit:** revenue × gross margin.
- **Sales:** leads × qualification × win rate × average deal size.
- **Subscription growth:** new recurring revenue + expansion − contraction − churn.
- **Capacity:** available hours × utilisation × productivity.
- **Working capital:** receivable days + inventory days − payable days.

## 12. Data-Quality Checklist

Flag any material issue involving:

- missing fields or periods;
- duplicate records;
- unreconciled totals;
- incorrect signs or units;
- mixed currencies;
- inconsistent date ranges;
- changed KPI definitions;
- incomplete segments;
- extreme values;
- small or biased samples;
- delayed postings;
- manual overrides; or
- use of averages that conceal material segments.

A data-quality issue should include its likely effect on the conclusion and a proposed validation step.

## 13. Root-Cause Analysis

### Step 1 — Define the gap

Write the problem as a measurable deviation:

`Actual outcome − Expected outcome, for a defined segment and period`

### Step 2 — Decompose

Break the outcome into drivers. For example:

`Profit = Revenue − Variable costs − Fixed costs`

`Revenue = Volume × Price × Mix effect`

### Step 3 — Segment

Locate where the change is concentrated: customer, product, geography, channel, cohort, team, supplier, or process stage.

### Step 4 — Test hypotheses

For each proposed cause, ask:

- What evidence supports it?
- What evidence would contradict it?
- Did it change before the outcome changed?
- Is it large enough to explain the gap?
- Is there an alternative explanation?

### Step 5 — Identify controllable causes

Distinguish:

- external cause;
- internal policy or decision;
- process failure;
- capability gap;
- temporary timing issue; and
- measurement problem.

### Step 6 — Correct and monitor

Recommend corrective action, owner, leading indicator, lagging outcome, and review date.

## 14. Dashboard Commentary Standard

A useful commentary answers four questions:

1. **What happened?**
2. **Why did it happen?**
3. **Why does it matter?**
4. **What should management do?**

Example:

> Revenue grew 5% quarter over quarter, but operating profit moved from a positive result to a loss. The deterioration was driven by a six-percentage-point decline in gross margin and higher marketing expenditure, while returns also increased. Management should tighten discount governance, investigate the return spike by product and channel, and pause low-return acquisition spending until unit economics recover.

# Part III — Market and Competitor Intelligence

## 15. Market Assessment

Assess only dimensions relevant to the decision:

- market definition and boundaries;
- customer segments and unmet needs;
- market size and growth;
- demand drivers;
- pricing and margin structure;
- channels and buying process;
- competitive intensity;
- regulation and barriers;
- required capabilities;
- entry economics; and
- principal risks.

### Market-sizing hierarchy

Prefer, in order:

1. verified company or industry data;
2. bottom-up calculation using customers × usage × price;
3. top-down estimate from credible market data; and
4. clearly labelled illustrative assumptions.

Distinguish:

- **TAM:** total theoretical demand;
- **SAM:** demand within the company’s served scope; and
- **SOM:** realistically obtainable share within the planning horizon.

## 16. Competitor Comparison

Use a consistent evidence period and compare:

| Dimension | Questions |
|---|---|
| Target customer | Which segments and use cases are prioritised? |
| Offering | What products, service levels, or bundles are provided? |
| Value proposition | What customer benefit is emphasised? |
| Pricing position | Premium, parity, value, or unclear? |
| Route to market | Direct, partner, marketplace, retail, or hybrid? |
| Strengths | Which capabilities or assets are difficult to match? |
| Weaknesses | Where is the offer or execution vulnerable? |
| Strategic moves | What recent launches, partnerships, expansion, or investment matter? |
| Threat level | How likely and severe is the competitive impact? |
| Response | Defend, differentiate, partner, imitate selectively, or avoid? |

Separate verified evidence from inference. Do not infer market leadership merely from brand visibility.

## 17. Customer Segmentation

Useful segmentation variables include:

- customer size;
- industry;
- geography;
- use case;
- behaviour or usage;
- profitability;
- growth potential;
- needs and willingness to pay;
- acquisition channel;
- retention risk; and
- cost to serve.

An actionable segment must be identifiable, measurable, reachable, economically meaningful, and associated with a different action.

# Part IV — Financial and Commercial Analysis

## 18. Core Measures

| Measure | Formula or interpretation |
|---|---|
| Revenue growth | `(Current revenue − Previous revenue) / Previous revenue` |
| Gross margin | `Gross profit / Revenue` |
| Operating margin | `Operating profit / Revenue` |
| Contribution margin | `(Revenue − Variable costs) / Revenue` |
| Budget variance | `Actual − Budget` |
| ROI | `(Total benefits − Total costs) / Total costs` |
| Payback period | `Initial investment / Annual net cash benefit` |
| Break-even volume | `Fixed costs / Contribution per unit` |
| Customer acquisition cost | `Relevant acquisition spend / New customers acquired` |
| Customer lifetime value | Use the company-approved model; otherwise label any simplified estimate. |

Use cash flows—not accounting profit alone—when assessing an investment. Apply discounting when timing is material and the necessary rate is available.

## 19. Business Case Structure

A concise business case should contain:

1. problem or opportunity;
2. strategic alignment;
3. baseline or status quo;
4. options considered;
5. financial assumptions;
6. costs and benefits by period;
7. ROI, payback, and NPV where relevant;
8. non-financial benefits;
9. delivery requirements;
10. risks and sensitivities;
11. recommendation; and
12. approval or decision required.

## 20. Scenario Analysis

Use at least three coherent scenarios:

- **Base:** most likely assumptions;
- **Upside:** favourable but plausible assumptions; and
- **Downside:** adverse but plausible assumptions.

For each scenario, specify:

- assumptions that change;
- financial or operational result;
- trigger or early-warning indicator;
- management response; and
- decision implication.

Avoid changing every assumption simultaneously without explaining the causal story.

## 21. Sensitivity Analysis

Test the few variables most likely to alter the decision, such as:

- sales volume;
- price;
- gross margin;
- implementation delay;
- adoption rate;
- churn;
- cost inflation;
- exchange rate; or
- discount rate.

State the break point at which the recommendation changes when it can be calculated.

# Part V — Decision, Risk, and Execution

## 22. Decision Memo Logic

A decision memo should answer:

- What must be decided?
- Why now?
- What facts are material?
- Which options were considered?
- What criteria were used?
- What is recommended and why?
- What are the principal risks?
- What must happen next?

State one recommended option. Do not leave the decision maker with an unranked menu unless the evidence genuinely cannot support a preference.

## 23. Risk Assessment

### Simple scoring

`Risk score = Likelihood × Impact`

Use a consistent scale, such as 1 to 5, and define the meaning of low, medium, and high.

For each material risk, record:

- risk event;
- cause;
- consequence;
- likelihood;
- impact;
- mitigation;
- contingency;
- early-warning indicator;
- owner; and
- review date.

Distinguish a **risk** (uncertain future event) from an **issue** (already occurring).

## 24. Impact–Effort Prioritisation

Classify actions as:

- **Quick wins:** high impact, low effort;
- **Major initiatives:** high impact, high effort;
- **Fill-ins:** low impact, low effort; and
- **Deprioritise:** low impact, high effort.

Then adjust for urgency, dependency, risk, and strategic alignment. A low-effort action is not automatically important.

## 25. 30–60–90-Day Execution Pattern

### Days 1–30: Diagnose and stabilise

- validate data and assumptions;
- stop immediate value leakage;
- assign owners;
- establish baseline KPIs; and
- resolve urgent decisions.

### Days 31–60: Implement and test

- launch priority actions;
- pilot uncertain interventions;
- monitor leading indicators;
- address capability gaps; and
- escalate material dependencies.

### Days 61–90: Scale and govern

- evaluate results;
- scale what works;
- stop or redesign weak initiatives;
- embed governance and reporting; and
- update the longer-term roadmap.

# Part VI — Quality Standard

## 26. Management-Ready Answer Checklist

Before presenting an answer, verify:

- The decision or objective is explicit.
- The reporting period and units are clear.
- Figures reconcile or discrepancies are flagged.
- Material comparisons are quantified.
- Facts, assumptions, and inferences are separated.
- The analysis identifies drivers, not only symptoms.
- Recommendations are prioritised and feasible.
- Owners, timing, and measures are included where useful.
- Risks and information gaps are visible.
- The answer is concise enough for the intended audience.

## 27. Common Failure Modes

Avoid:

- framework dumping;
- unsupported causal claims;
- percentages without base values;
- averages that conceal important segments;
- forecasts without assumptions;
- recommendations that ignore capacity or cash;
- long lists without prioritisation;
- confusing activity with outcome;
- treating revenue growth as proof of value creation; and
- presenting false precision when evidence is limited.

A strong answer is not the longest answer. It is the answer that makes the business situation, decision, evidence, risk, and next action clear.
