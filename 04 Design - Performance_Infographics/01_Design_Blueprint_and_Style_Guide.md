# Performance Infographics — Design Blueprint and Style Guide

**Project:** Performance Infographics  
**Primary example:** NorthBridge Business Services Ltd.  
**Primary format:** Single-page landscape infographic  
**Recommended canvas:** 16:9, approximately 1920 × 1080 pixels  
**Audience:** Executives, managers, analysts, and business teams

---

## 1. Design objective

Create a professional performance infographic that allows a senior manager to understand the following within one minute:

1. Is the business growing?
2. Is performance above or below target?
3. Which financial, customer, and operational issues matter most?
4. Which strategic initiatives are on track or at risk?
5. What should management do next?

The infographic must communicate a management story rather than display every available number.

---

## 2. Executive message

The design should make this message immediately clear:

> NorthBridge continued to grow in Q2 2026, but revenue remained below target and profitability weakened because of margin pressure and slower cost improvement. Customer advocacy improved, while retention and delivery reliability declined. Management should prioritise margin recovery, account retention, and operating discipline.

This message should guide the hierarchy, chart selection, and management callouts.

---

## 3. Recommended information architecture

Use the following top-to-bottom structure.

### A. Header

Include:

- title: **Q2 2026 Corporate Performance**
- company: **NorthBridge Business Services Ltd.**
- subtitle: **Growth continued, but margin and retention require management action**
- reporting note: **Fictional training data**

### B. Executive KPI strip

Display six KPI cards:

1. Revenue
2. Gross margin
3. Operating profit
4. Customer retention
5. Net Promoter Score
6. On-time delivery

Each card should show:

- current result;
- target;
- variance;
- simple status;
- very short interpretation.

### C. Performance story

Use no more than three primary visualisations:

1. revenue actual versus target by quarter;
2. gross-margin and operating-profit trend;
3. regional or business-unit performance comparison.

### D. Customer and operations panel

Show a compact comparison of:

- new customers;
- retention;
- Net Promoter Score;
- support resolution;
- on-time delivery;
- process-cycle time;
- first-time-right rate;
- employee productivity.

### E. Strategic execution and risk panel

Show:

- four strategic initiatives with progress and status;
- five enterprise risks with severity;
- the three highest-priority management actions.

### F. Closing takeaway

End with a concise statement:

> Protect growth by restoring margin, stabilising key-account retention, and improving delivery discipline.

---

## 4. Layout blueprint

Use a balanced landscape grid.

```text
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Header: title, subtitle, reporting period, fictional-data note      │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ KPI 1 │ KPI 2 │ KPI 3 │ KPI 4 │ KPI 5 │ KPI 6                      │
├───────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Revenue and profit trends │ Regional / business-unit comparison     │
├───────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Customer and operations   │ Initiatives, risks, and priorities      │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Executive takeaway and three management actions                     │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Recommended proportions:

- header: 10–12% of page height;
- KPI strip: 18–20%;
- analytical middle: 40–45%;
- execution and takeaway area: 25–30%.

---

## 5. Visual hierarchy

Use four information levels.

### Level 1 — Primary conclusion

The title, subtitle, and headline takeaway must be the most visible elements.

### Level 2 — Executive KPIs

KPI values and status must be visible from presentation distance.

### Level 3 — Supporting evidence

Charts and comparisons should explain the KPI story.

### Level 4 — Detail

Definitions, short notes, risk descriptions, and source statements should be smaller but still readable.

Do not make every label equally prominent.

---

## 6. Corporate visual system

### Recommended palette

| Purpose | Colour | Hex |
|---|---|---|
| Primary corporate | Deep navy | `#0B1F3A` |
| Primary accent | Executive blue | `#1F5FBF` |
| Secondary accent | Teal | `#0F8B8D` |
| Positive / on track | Green | `#2E7D32` |
| Caution / attention | Amber | `#F59E0B` |
| Adverse / high risk | Red | `#C62828` |
| Main background | Off-white | `#F7F9FC` |
| Panel background | White | `#FFFFFF` |
| Secondary text | Slate grey | `#5B6573` |
| Divider lines | Light grey | `#D8DEE8` |

Use navy and blue for structure. Reserve green, amber, and red for status.

### Status rules

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Green | At or above target, or initiative clearly on track |
| Amber | Moderate underperformance, uncertainty, or attention required |
| Red | Material underperformance, delay, or high enterprise risk |
| Grey | Context, neutral information, or unavailable comparison |

Do not use colour alone. Add words such as **On track**, **Attention**, **Below target**, **Delayed**, or **High risk**.

---

## 7. Typography

Use a clean sans-serif family available in the design environment.

Recommended hierarchy:

- title: bold, 30–38 pt equivalent;
- subtitle: medium or semibold, 16–20 pt;
- KPI value: bold, 24–32 pt;
- section heading: bold, 16–20 pt;
- body text: 11–14 pt;
- source note: 9–10 pt.

Writing rules:

- use sentence case;
- avoid long all-capital headings;
- keep section headings short;
- use one main font family;
- use bold weight to create hierarchy rather than many font sizes;
- avoid paragraphs longer than three lines.

---

## 8. KPI-card design

Each KPI card should contain:

```text
KPI name
Current result
Target and variance
Status label
One-line interpretation
```

Example:

```text
Operating profit
$6.4m
$1.1m below target
Below target
Margin pressure offset revenue growth
```

KPI cards should have equal dimensions and consistent spacing.

Avoid:

- oversized icons;
- excessive decimals;
- unexplained arrows;
- multiple messages inside one card.

---

## 9. Chart-selection guidance

### Revenue trend

Use a grouped column chart or line chart showing:

- actual revenue;
- target revenue;
- four quarters from Q3 2025 to Q2 2026.

### Profitability trend

Use a compact dual-line or small-multiple visual showing:

- gross margin percentage;
- operating profit in millions.

Because the units differ, clearly separate the axes or use two aligned mini charts. Do not imply that the two scales are directly comparable.

### Regional or business-unit comparison

Use horizontal bars or a compact comparison table. Show:

- actual revenue;
- target attainment;
- gross margin;
- status.

### Initiative progress

Use progress bars with status labels. Do not imply false precision beyond the stated percentage.

### Risk display

Use a ranked risk list or matrix. Show risk name, severity, and management response.

---

## 10. Icon and illustration rules

Use simple line or filled icons only when they improve scanning.

Suitable icon themes:

- revenue;
- profit;
- customers;
- service;
- operations;
- strategy;
- risk;
- action.

Keep icon style consistent. Avoid decorative photography, 3D illustrations, cartoons, or unrelated imagery.

---

## 11. Content-editing rules

The design should use concise business language.

Prefer:

- **Revenue grew 5.2% quarter on quarter**
- **Gross margin fell 0.6 percentage points**
- **Retention remains 1.8 points below target**

Avoid:

- long explanations;
- repeated numbers;
- generic phrases such as “continue to monitor” without an owner or action;
- unsupported causal claims;
- promotional language.

Use:

- percentage (`%`) for rates and relative change;
- percentage points (`pp`) for the difference between two percentages;
- millions (`m`) for financial figures;
- one decimal place only where useful.

---

## 12. Data-integrity rules

The design must:

- reproduce all source values accurately;
- distinguish target variance from prior-period change;
- label currency as US dollars;
- keep reporting periods consistent;
- avoid truncating axes in a misleading way;
- label estimated or inferred information;
- show the fictional-data note;
- avoid adding values not contained in the source file.

Where the source does not prove a cause, use language such as:

- “likely contributor”;
- “requires validation”;
- “associated with”;
- “management hypothesis”.

---

## 13. Accessibility and readability

The final design should:

- maintain high contrast;
- use readable font sizes;
- avoid red-green-only distinctions;
- include text status labels;
- use adequate white space;
- avoid dense tables;
- maintain a logical reading order;
- remain understandable in greyscale where possible;
- use direct chart labels instead of relying only on legends.

---

## 14. Optional portrait adaptation

After the landscape version is complete, a portrait adaptation may use:

```text
Header
Executive message
Six KPI cards in two columns
Financial trends
Customer and operations
Initiatives
Risks
Management actions
Source note
```

Do not merely compress the landscape design. Reflow and simplify the content for the narrower page.

---

## 15. Acceptance criteria

The design is complete only when all of the following are true:

### Content

- [ ] the main management message is immediately visible;
- [ ] six executive KPIs are accurate;
- [ ] every chart supports a management conclusion;
- [ ] initiatives, risks, and actions are present;
- [ ] the fictional-data note is visible.

### Visual quality

- [ ] the layout is balanced and uncluttered;
- [ ] the KPI strip is consistent;
- [ ] status colours follow the defined rules;
- [ ] typography is readable;
- [ ] alignment and spacing are consistent;
- [ ] no decorative element competes with the data.

### Decision usefulness

- [ ] the design explains what happened;
- [ ] it shows where performance differs from target;
- [ ] it identifies the most important implications;
- [ ] it states three prioritised management actions;
- [ ] it can be understood in approximately one minute.
